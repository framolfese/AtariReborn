Welcome on ATARI's games history!

This is an interactive Timeline that shows the evolution of arcade games.

  • Tennis for two

    In 1958 Willy Higinbotham of Brookhaven National Laboratory, noticing that his students did not take care to the subject as much as they should have, he created Tennis for Two, the first game that had to simulate the physical laws of a tennis match. The means used was an oscilloscope.

  • Heathkit IG-62

    In the 1960s Ralph Baer was one of the first television engineers in the world and worked at Sanders and Associates (a company that developed air and submarine radar systems). In 1966, during a business trip, he wrote down on some notepads some sketches and drawings trying to schematize some thoughts on how you could interact (playing) with a normal TV at home. These notes convinced Sanders to develop the project and to file the patents of that idea as early as 1966, and the company encouraged Baer to continue its development, putting a duly equipped room at the disposal of the engineer himself. After a few months Baer had a bright dot that could be moved at will on the screen of a normal TV. The Heathkit IG-62 alignment generator, which he himself built to test televisions, now made it possible to move the white dot on a black screen.

  • Atari ...born

    In 1972 Nolan Bushnell, a young engineer who worked in Ampex (a company that designed integrated circuits and magnetic tapes for video recording), left his job and founded Atari. In just a few years, Bushnell and its new company set themselves the task of replacing pinball machines with coin-op video games. In the first months of production (we are at the beginning of 1973) Atari sold about 2300 units of the coin-op Pong. A game very similar to the ping-pong of Baer for the Odissey. The fact is that the Atari coin-op, despite being marketed and appeared to the public after the launch of the Odyssey console, has gone down in history as the first video game.

  • Space Invaders

    Space Invaders is a 1978 arcade videogame developed by Toshihiro Nishikado. It was originally produced by Taito, and licensed for production in the US at the Midway Games division of Bally Technologies. Although it is very simple by today's standards, it was one of the most influential videogames of its generation: the game generated a turnover of 500 million dollars in a few years and its publication actually decreed a period of great fortune for videogames, defined later as the golden age of arcade video games.

  • Pac Man e il 256

    This level is considered, due to a bug, the last level of Pac-Man. Theoretically the game can continue indefinitely, given that each level that follows is virtually identical to the one just completed, but arrived at level n. 256 the scene changes substantially, due to a bug in the function that draws fruit in the lower bar of the screen, where the indication of the current level appears. There should not be more than seven fruits in the line, because the code deals with drawing - just in case the number of the level is less than 8 - as many fruits as the current level. Since the level number is recorded on a single byte, completed 255º (in hexadecimal "FF"), the function reads from the hexadecimal number 100 (ie 256 in decimal) only the "00".

  • Space War

    In 1961 six young scientists of MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology) found out how to move dots of light on a PDP-1 (Programmed Data Processor-1, the first of PDP series of digial equipment that entered in the bussines during 1960). Space War was born, the first program that we can call a videogame. But due to the complexity of the project and the high costs of development on the PDP-1, we had to wait until the end of 1973 when Space War reached the general public as a board game.

  • Magnavox Odissey

    Although Atari in the early 1980s made black dots known in the world on white screen, the technology invented by Baer was the first that was used in the early seventies for the creation of the first video game console in history: the Magnavox Odyssey. The prototype of the Magnavox Odyssey (called in the Sanders Brown Box or just Odyssey) was ready in 1970 and the series marketing began in Christmas 1972 with a ping-pong game. It was basically a ball (a white dot on a black screen) that was horizontally riveted on the TV screen by two rackets (two white sticks on a black screen), controllable by the players (maximum 2) with two controllers (currently we would call them joypad) with wheels, which allowed to move the rackets vertically. In the launch year the Odyssey sold over 165,000 units, and thanks to an extensive advertising campaign, the only and only home console for video games, sold in the second year (we are at the end of 1973) an additional 200,000 boxes.

  • Pong

    Atari's Pong was a game intended for public places and not for the four walls of the house, so even those who did not know the existence of video games had the first contact with them thanks to Pong. For this reason Atari entered the collective imagination as the house that had generated that new world of electronic entertainment, even though it was only in 1976 that Atari (thanks also to the collaboration of Activision) began marketing its version of the home console, which, for reasons of a different and better advertising capacity of Atari (more than for an actual superior quality) immediately supplanted the Odyssey of the Sanders / Magnavox.

  • Snake

    The genre of videogames to which the various Snake belonged originated with the arcade Blockade videogame of 1976, which introduced the game mechanics with ever longer solid contrails. In particular his second sequel, Hustle (1977), introduced the game also in single player and goals to collect to get points. The first version designed for home computers, called Worm, was developed in 1979 by F. Seger (Germany) on the TRS-80 computer, followed closely by a version for the Commodore VIC-20. A version of Blockade called Hustler was then developed by the Milton Bradley Company for the TI-99 / 4A in 1980.

  • Donkey Kong & Jumpman

    Donkey Kong is an arcade video game platform developed by Nintendo and released in arcades in 1981. It was later converted for numerous types of computers and consoles, as well as Game & Watch. In the game the famous characters Mario and Donkey Kong appear for the first time. In the videogame Donkey Kong Mario was called Jumpman (the "jumper"), a name that two years later would have inspired the platformer of the same name Jumpman for Atari 8-bit and Commodore 64. In Japan the character was known as Mr. Video, but during American localization of Donkey Kong Nintendo of America founder Minoru Arakawa decided to call him Mario for the resemblance (as well as symbolic thanksgiving) to Mario Segale (1934-2018), owner of the first Nintendo plant in the United States of America, in in the eighties the headquarters were located.
